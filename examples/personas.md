# Target Audience & Personas: Examples & Templates

This guide helps you define who your product is for and create meaningful user personas.

---

## What are Personas?

Personas are fictional representations of your target users based on research, data, and assumptions. They help you:
- Make user-centered design decisions
- Prioritize features
- Write appropriate copy
- Choose the right channels
- Align team on user needs

**Format:** Name + Role + Goals + Frustrations + Context

## Example 1: Productivity App (Taskly)

### Persona 1: Maya (Freelance UX Designer)

**Demographics:**
- Age: 29
- Location: Remote (Lisbon, Portugal)
- Occupation: Freelance UX Designer
- Income: $60-80K/year
- Education: Bachelor's in Design

**Goals:**
- Stay organized across 5+ client projects simultaneously
- Meet deadlines without feeling overwhelmed
- Separate personal tasks from work tasks
- Minimize time spent on planning, maximize time creating

**Frustrations:**
- Loses track of small tasks across multiple tools (Notion, Trello, email)
- Misses deadlines when juggling too many clients
- Overwhelmed by notifications from productivity apps
- Doesn't have time to learn complex project management tools

**Motivations:**
- Wants to feel in control of workload
- Values simplicity over features
- Needs mobile access while traveling
- Prefers visual organization (lists, boards)

**Devices:**
- MacBook Pro (primary)
- iPhone 14 (on-the-go)
- iPad (occasional)

**Tech Savviness:** High
**Frequency of Use:** Daily (multiple times per day)

**Quote:**
> "I need a tool that just works. I don't have time to configure a complex system—I need to capture tasks fast and move on."

**How Taskly Helps:**
- Fast task capture (< 3 seconds)
- Offline sync for travel
- Visual daily overview
- No learning curve

## Creating Your Personas

### Step 1: Research

**Sources:**
- User interviews
- Surveys
- Analytics data
- Customer support conversations
- Competitor reviews

**Don't have users yet?**
- Interview people who fit your target audience
- Use assumptions (validate later)
- Study competitors' users

---

### Step 2: Identify Patterns

**Group users by:**
- Goals (what they want to achieve)
- Frustrations (what blocks them)
- Behavior (how they use similar products)
- Context (when/where they use products)

**Not by demographics alone!**
Age and location matter less than goals and frustrations.

---

### Step 3: Create 2-4 Personas

**Don't create too many** (hard to design for).

**Focus on:**
- Primary persona (most important)
- Secondary persona (different needs)
- Edge persona (extreme use case)

---

### Step 4: Give Them Life

**Include:**
- Name and photo (stock or AI-generated)
- Age, occupation, location
- Goals (what they're trying to achieve)
- Frustrations (what blocks them)
- Motivations (why they care)
- Devices and tech savviness
- Usage frequency
- Quote (in their voice)

## Persona Anti-Patterns

### ❌ Don't Do This:

**1. Too Many Demographics, No Goals**
```
"28-year-old female from New York who likes yoga"
```
→ Missing: What does she want to achieve?

**2. Generic Personas**
```
"Busy professional who wants to be productive"
```
→ Too vague. Be specific.

**3. Based Only on Assumptions**
→ Validate with real user research

**4. Too Many Personas**
→ 8 personas = designing for no one

**5. Personas Gathering Dust**
→ Reference them in every product decision

## Using Personas

### In Design Decisions
"Would Maya find this feature helpful?"

### In Prioritization
"Darius needs reporting more than Jess does → prioritize for Pro users"

### In Copywriting
"Write in Jess's voice: gentle, non-judgmental"

### In Marketing
"Target channels where Maya hangs out: Dribbble, Behance, Twitter"

## Updating Personas

**Personas should evolve.**

**When to update:**
- After user research
- When product pivots
- When you discover new user segments
- Every 6-12 months

---

## Lightweight Alternative: Jobs to Be Done

**If personas feel too heavy, try JTBD:**

**Format:**
> "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

**Example:**
> "When I'm overwhelmed by tasks, I want to quickly capture them, so I can stop worrying and focus on one thing."

**Benefits:**
- Focuses on job, not person
- More flexible
- Easier to validate
